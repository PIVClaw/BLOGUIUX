When people talk about “memory” for a personal assistant, the classic trap is looking for one magical storage layer that does everything. In practice, that is neither realistic nor desirable. A solid memory system separates needs that do not share the same constraints.

In my Jarvis setup on Raspberry Pi 5, memory is built on three complementary layers.

## 1. Structured memory: SQLite

The first layer is structured memory. It lives in `jarvis.db` and stores what must remain clean, queryable, and coherent over time.

Typically, this is where you expect to keep:

- entities,
- facts,
- relationships,
- commitments,
- tasks,
- profiles,
- and conversation metadata.

This layer acts as the application backbone.

## 2. Semantic memory: Qdrant

The second layer is vector retrieval. Its role is not perfect structure, but fast access to information that is semantically close to a given request.

Qdrant handles:

- vector storage,
- similarity search,
- context retrieval,
- and better relevance for notes, memories, and related documents.

## 3. Human-readable memory: the Obsidian vault

The third layer is often underestimated: memory should also remain readable by a human. That is exactly what the Obsidian vault provides.

It contains folders such as `Conversations`, `Knowledge`, `Memories`, `Projects`, `Daily`, and `Scratchpad`.

## Why not centralize everything?

Because the three needs are different.

- SQLite is excellent for structured objects.
- Qdrant is excellent for semantic retrieval.
- Obsidian is excellent for readability and human editing.

Trying to put everything into one tool usually means making mediocre compromises everywhere.
